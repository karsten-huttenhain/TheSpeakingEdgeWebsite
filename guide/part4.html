Part Four

Difficult Moments, Handled.

Interruptions, ambush questions, hostile framings, and stage fright — with calm, specific scripts you can actually use.

Getting interrupted is one of the most exhausting parts of meetings for introverted professionals. There's a third option beyond shrinking or escalating: reclaim the floor calmly.

Reclaim phrases

  • "Hold on — let me finish my thought, and then I want to hear you."
  • "I wasn't quite done. Give me thirty seconds to land this and then it's yours."
  • "Sorry, can I just complete the sentence I was on?"
  • "One moment — the part I was getting to is the part that matters."

Senior people are trusted not because they always know the answer, but because of how they behave when they don't.

The four senior responses

  • "I don't know yet. Here's how I'd find out."
  • "Good question — I want to give you a real answer rather than a guess. I'll come back to you by [time]."
  • "I don't have data on that, but my read on it is… I'd want to verify before we act on it."
  • "That's outside what I've looked at closely. [Person] would know the specifics. What I can speak to is…"
The trust shortcut

Get a reputation for saying "I don't know yet" crisply, instead of bluffing. Within six months you will be one of the most trusted people in your organisation.

Sometimes a question isn't really about the work. It's loaded.

1.
Don't match the energy. Slow down. Drop your voice. Take half a breath.
2.
Address content, not subtext. "What I'm hearing you ask is whether [X]. The answer is [Y]."
3.
Name it, only if needed. "I want to make sure we're still discussing the work and not something else."
4.
Take it offline. "Something felt off between us, and I'd rather have the conversation than wonder."

Stage fright is not a sign that you are bad at this. It is a sign that you care.

1.
Reframe the sensation — "I'm terrified" and "I'm revved up" are the same body, two different labels. Try the second.
2.
Land in your body, not in your head — Move your attention to a physical anchor: feet on the floor, weight in your seat.
3.
Use the exhale, not the inhale — Four seconds in, six seconds out, twice. Your heart rate drops measurably within thirty seconds.
4.
Have a first sentence you can say on autopilot — Memorise your opening line until you could say it half-asleep.
5.
Make the audience the point, not yourself — What do they need? What are they worried about? The same nervous energy, redirected outward, becomes generosity.
